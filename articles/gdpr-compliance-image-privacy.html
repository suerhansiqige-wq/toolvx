GDPR Compliance for Images: Complete Legal Guide

Understanding your legal obligations when publishing photos in the EU

Are Faces Considered Personal Data Under GDPR?

Yes! Under GDPR Article 4, facial images are considered biometric data and qualify as personal data. This means you must have a lawful basis (usually explicit consent) before publishing someone's photo.

Key Point:

Even if you don't name someone, their face alone can identify them, making it personal data under GDPR.

Company website team photos YES - Written consent
Event photography (public) Maybe - Depends on context

Blurred/anonymized images NO - Not personal data

Lawful Bases Beyond Consent

Consent is the most cited GDPR basis for publishing photos, but it is not the only one—and it is often the weakest for ongoing website use because individuals can withdraw consent at any time (Article 7(3)). Legitimate interest (Article 6(1)(f)) may apply when you document a public protest, report news, or publish CCTV stills for theft prevention, provided you balance your interest against the data subject's rights and document the assessment. Contractual necessity covers ID verification during onboarding. Legal obligation covers regulatory filings that require identity proof.

For each photo category on your site—team page, case studies, event galleries—record the lawful basis in your Record of Processing Activities (ROPA). If you rely on consent, store signed release forms with scope (web, print, social), duration, and withdrawal instructions. When consent is withdrawn, you must remove or anonymize the image promptly; keeping a blurred version may suffice if re-identification is no longer reasonably likely. When Blur Counts as Anonymization

GDPR Recital 26 states that anonymized data falls outside scope only if identification is impossible or requires disproportionate effort. Light Gaussian blur on a distinctive face in a small team photo may not qualify—colleagues can still guess who it is from context, clothing, or tattoos. Effective anonymization for compliance typically requires mosaic pixelation or solid blackout covering the full face including hairline and ears, plus removal of name badges and desk nameplates in the same frame.

Document your anonymization standard in internal policy: which effect type, minimum coverage area, and verification step (second reviewer at 200% zoom). For high-risk biometric processing, consult your Data Protection Officer before publishing. Responding to Erasure Requests (Article 17)

When someone exercises the right to erasure, you must delete their personal data across systems unless an exemption applies (freedom of expression, legal claims, public interest archiving). For marketing websites, that means removing the photo from the CMS, CDN caches, social mirrors, and backup snapshots scheduled for restoration. If complete deletion breaks a historical record (annual report PDF), replace identifiable images with anonymized versions rather than leaving identifiable copies in archived PDFs.

Maintain an erasure log: request date, identity verified, assets removed, anonymized replacements uploaded, and confirmation sent to the data subject within one month (extendable to three with notice). Pair image handling with broader document hygiene from our Business Document Security guide so PDF attachments and scanned forms receive the same rigor as website hero images. Proactive anonymization at capture time costs minutes; reactive firefighting after a complaint costs weeks and regulator attention.
