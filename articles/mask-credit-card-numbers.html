How to Mask Credit Card Numbers in Images

Essential guide to protecting payment information in photos

Why Credit Card Security in Photos Matters

With contactless payments and digital wallets, we often photograph credit cards for records, receipts, or verification. However, sharing these images without proper masking can lead to immediate fraud. Criminals can use visible card numbers, expiry dates, and CVV codes to make unauthorized purchases.

🚨 NEVER Share These Visible:

  • • Full 16-digit card number
  • • CVV/CVC code (3 digits on back)
  • • Expiry date combined with card number
  • • Cardholder's full name with number
  • • Billing address with card details

Proper Masking Techniques

The Safe Way to Display Cards

When you must show a card (for verification, tutorials, etc.), follow these rules:

  • ▼ Show only first 6 and last 4 digits: 123456******7890
  • ▼ Completely cover CVV with black box
  • ▼ Obscure expiry date partially
  • ▼ Use solid black masks (not semi-transparent)

Common Situations Where Card Photos Leak

Card images escape privacy in predictable ways. Rental applications ask for "proof of payment method" and tenants photograph the front and back without masking the CVV. Marketplace sellers post wallet photos to prove they can pay, exposing full PAN and expiry. Expense-report apps encourage snapping receipts that include the last four digits on the same page as a visible card in the background. Support chats for subscription services often request a screenshot of the card on file—exactly the scenario fraudsters simulate in phishing emails.

Treat every card photo as if it will be indexed by search engines or scraped from a compromised cloud backup. Before attaching to email, Slack, or a ticket portal, open the image in toolvx, draw blackout boxes over digits 7–12 (the middle eight), fully cover the CVV strip on the reverse, and mask the expiry if the full number is visible anywhere in the frame. PCI-DSS rules prohibit storing CVV after authorization—there is no legitimate reason to transmit an unmasked CVV in an image.

PCI-Aligned Masking: What to Show and What to Hide

Payment card industry guidance allows displaying the first six and last four digits (BIN + last four) for dispute resolution—format: 411111******1111. Never show the full primary account number, magnetic-stripe data, or CVV in any customer-facing or internal wiki screenshot. For virtual cards used in QA environments, apply the same rules: test PANs still train fraud models and may belong to live BIN ranges.

Choose blackout (solid fill) for all numeric fields on cards—blur and mosaic can leave recoverable patterns under magnification on glossy card surfaces with embossed numbers. Extend each selection box two to three pixels beyond the digit edges to catch shadows and reflections. After export, zoom to 200% and confirm no digit strokes remain visible. Our FAQ compares mosaic, blur, and blackout if you are redacting related documents in the same session.

Beyond the Card: Redacting Receipt and Wallet Shots

Wallet photos often include driver's licenses, transit passes with serial numbers, and insurance cards with member IDs—all in one frame. Scan the entire image, not just the payment card. Redact barcodes on the back of loyalty cards; many encode account identifiers scannable from a photo. If you are submitting proof of purchase, crop or blackout unrelated items in the wallet spread before sharing.
